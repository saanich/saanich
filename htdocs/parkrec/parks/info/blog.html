Ring that Bell – Confessions of a New Commuter Cyclist

As we approach the annual Bike to Work Week (BTWW), I thought it was a good time to reflect on my last year as a new commuter cyclist.  For the 10 years that I have been with Saanich Parks, I have always participated in BTWW. Then as soon as it was over I’d return to my car. You see, Saanich requires me to provide my own transportation for business. So my excuse was “I need my car for my job”. But last year, I decided to keep riding.

Over the past year I have learned a great deal of things. Some has been about me & some about trail use/users. I have also rediscovered connections to my youth & the environment. I also learned that it can really rain here but you dry out (eventually).

For better or worse, here is my list of what I have learned:

  • Great trail system - OK I already knew that & it’s my job to say that but its true. We have over 100 km of trails in Saanich. The access to the CRD Galloping Goose & Lochside Trail as spines to our system is fantastic. I am lucky that for my commute I use off road trails for almost all of it.
  • It’s Social – The use of our trails for both commuting and recreation is a social activity. People walk 2 abreast or cycle side by side to socialize. They are talking about their day, their plans, the weather or what ever but it’s a social experience. However, on bike or foot, when you pass others adjust to single file. 1 trail user going in each direction at the same time makes it easy & comfortable for all
  • I love it – I enjoy my ride to & from work; even in the rain. When I started I resisted buying a bunch of stuff &/or spending a lot of money. The rack & panniers were a good investment. Plus I’ve even dropped a few pounds & feel better.
  • Enjoy the ride – The commute offers me a chance to see things to smell things & enjoy things I never really could in the car. The micro-climate of the cool wind coming down the Lochside Trail to the Switch Bridge or the view of the Olympic Mtns on a clear day makes the ride enjoyable too.
  • Share the Trail Saanich – It’s our trail etiquette campaign & we need to keep getting this message out.  To ‘share’ means we all have a right to use & enjoy the trail. A few simple, common-sense considerations will make your trail-travel safer and more enjoyable for everyone. Show consideration for others.
  • Are you a car, a bike or what? – Drivers get frustrated when a cyclist switches about who they are as cyclists. Sometimes cyclists act like cars the next moment they are on the sidewalk like a pedestrian. I can see why some drivers get angry.
  • Keep right (except to pass) – We want everyone to do this. A multi-use trail is not a road & we don’t want pedestrians walking on the left facing the oncoming traffic. If everyone did this we’d end up with 4 abreast in 2 directions on each side of the trail.  If folks keep right, warn others when they pass we can all share.
  • Warn others when passing– I don’t know how many times I’ve been passed by someone (either when I’m on bike or when walking) whizzing by in silence. Just give us a friendly warning so we know you are there & passing, no surprises. By the way, the warning is not meant to signal “clear the deck I’m coming through” nor does it mean pedestrians need to jump out of the way. It should be made early enough that all can adjust & continue to enjoy the trail.
  • Slow down – Control your speed, & yield to slower users.  Some of the conflict on the trails exists from the speed of some cyclists. Don’t sweat; enjoy the view.
  • Control & clean up after your pet – Pets are an important part of people lives. Walking your dog on the trail is a very legitimate trail use. But I know that I always feel safer with a leashed dogs but those retractable leashes aren’t very good if the owner is on 1 side & the dog is in the ditch on the other. Remember dog droppings aren’t fun for anyone on the trails. Some parks/trails have leash regulations so be sure to check. http://www.saanich.ca/parkrec/parks/parks/dog.html
  • Be Seen – Lights, bright colours, reflectors all help trail users see each other. This goes for cyclists as well as pedestrians. I know in the dark winter months I have come upon many pedestrians that I could not see until my headlight showed me their outline. But those that have a light or a reflector I can spot well before the close call.
  • Respect – The environment and neighbours, keep to the trail.Maybe this is really a key message just like SHARE. If we RESPECT that everyone has a right to the trail maybe it’s that much easier to SHARE.

I have really enjoyed my first year as a commuter cyclist & plan to continue. I’d love to hear about your experiences & opinions on my observations as well as our Share the Trail Saanich program. Look for us at the Saanich Hall Celebration Station during BTWW again this year.

Looking back; looking forward

Each year we like to pause & think about what we have accomplished. Sometimes its sort of a ‘what did we do again’ but usually its wow we did all that.

2012 highlights:

Pulling Together – Our natural area management program continues to grow. The work we are able to do removing invasive species & restoring our natural parks is fantastic. Our program has grown to over 40 active sites with over 100 registered volunteers.  We also have up to 500+ on special projects.

http://www.saanich.ca/parkrec/parks/natural/index.html

Mount View Park -   This Park was created by the development of the former Mt View School site at 3814 Carey Road (now referred to as Mount View Heights). It is .54 ha (1.33 acres) in size, and is located in the Carey Local Area. The park includes unstructured open space, a playground and picnic area, a seating plaza with shrub and perennial beds, naturalistic features, trees, and pedestrian circulation links.

http://www.saanich.ca/parkrec/parks/projects/current/mtcarey.html

Some recent parks additions include: Doris Page, Harvest Lane, Browning, Mount Douglas & Gorge.

http://www.saanich.ca/parkrec/parks/projects/mt-doug/index.html

September 21, 2012
Changing Seasons

As fall approaches our programs & priorities change.

However, the following is a list of some of our current activities & successes:

Majestic Park – The old play equipment is being replaced with a new playground with an emphasis on accessibility and inclusiveness. Work will begin in late September, including planting shade trees, new furniture, and improvements to drainage and pathways.

http://saanich.ca/parkrec/parks/projects/current/majestic.html

Whitehead Park – While it received a bit of a mixed reaction the new multi-purpose dock indicated in the approved Park Concept Plan has been installed, and will complement the new playground, trails, and riparian restoration work done in the last 2 years. All permits have been secured, and the dock was installed under the supervision of a Qualified Environmental Professional to ensure no negative impacts to the environment result. Additionally this summer, parks staff with the help of some volunteers at removed the invasive Yellow Flag Iris. Over 120 square metres of iris were removed from the park shoreline. This was a difficult work as the rhizomes needed to be hand dug and removed to a compost facility. The area has now been covered with coco mat and reseeded with fall rye to control erosion.

 

The dock will enhance general recreation by increasing access to the lake for wading and viewing, and allow easy launching for carry-in watercraft.

Interpretive Signs – In partnership with the Saanich Volunteer Services Society, the interpretive sign and notice board was installed for their September 20th anniversary celebration. One side of the kiosk includes a map of Cedar Hill Park and Golf Course including the McCrae House. The other side of the kiosk is a notice board for the Society.

In partnership with the Gorge Tillicum Community Association, an interpretive sign will be installed by mid October at Gorge Park that talks about the history of the park and Gorge Waterway.

Completed signs to date include the Camosun Community Association notice board and map at Browning Park, the heritage interpretive sign at Doris Page Park and the map sign at Harvest Lane Park.

Directional Signs - Directional route marking signs will be installed this fall along the Colquitz River Trail from Gorge Road at Dysart through to Lindsay Street adjacent to Brydon Park. As the Colquitz River Trail is part of the Centennial Trail family of trails – it will be marked with the same style of sign. Staff are also currently working on updating all the small map kiosks throughout Mt Douglas Park with additional large kiosks to be placed at the parking lot locations.

Blenkinsop Trailhead - Parking lot construction is well underway with paving scheduled for September. Paving for this 9-car parking lot will be with permeable asphalt. A bike rack, interpretive sign and map will also be installed at the trail head. Staff have worked closely with a Garry oak ecosystem biologist and the Friends of Mount Douglas Park to select the trail route that will lead up from the parking lot to a fantastic viewpoint and then lead further into the park where it will meet with the Tod Trail. The trail will be approximately 450m in length and will be marked a black diamond route (most difficult). The Mercer Trail head at Blenkinsop Road will remain although de-emphasized by relocating the Mt Douglas Park sign to the entrance of the new parking area.

Douglas Creek Restoration – In May of 2012 Saanich Parks hired D.R. Clough Consulting to conduct a habitat and riparian assessment for Douglas Creek. We now have a five year plan to restore fish habitat. In late August and early September Parks staff along with consultant implemented the prescribed recommendations for the creek below Ash road. We have also repaired some small tributaries within the park which have been depositing silt within the creek. Work will continue for the next five years to fully implement the plan.

Clay Courts - Earlier this year the District of Saanich was contacted by a not for profit group called the Cedar Hill Clay Court Tennis Society to discuss the possibility of installing an outdoor clay court tennis facility.  The proposed location is on the north side of the Cedar Hill Recreation Centre in Cedar Hill Park. The society has met with key community groups, attended local events and had ongoing discussions with Saanich Parks and Recreation staff to explain the proposal and to learn about key issues associated with the proposed facility.

The Society, which would fund the facility, has collected some community feedback that has been considered into the design of the facility. The Society would now like to move forward with its proposal. Saanich Parks and Recreation staff will be hosting an open house on October 2nd to obtain public feedback on the proposal.

http://saanich.ca/parkrec/parks/projects/open.html

SOS Parks – Share our Saanich Parks

Not to be confused with save our souls but Share our Saanich Parks is a concept I have been ‘bugging’ the staff about over the past few weeks.

What do I mean by that? We ask people to Share the Trail, we want to share information, we ask users to share spaces in parks tax payers feel they own a share of their parks. There are lots of ways this concept of SHARING can apply. I actually want it to be all of this & more!

Many years ago I read a book by Robert Fulghurn, All I Really Need to Know I Learned in Kindergarten. I remember that part of this book mentions sharing. I was surprised to be reminded that the #1 thing he says he learned was: Share everything. The rest of the list, 14 in all, is pretty good too. One was also about to Live a balanced life.

The Oxford dictionary states that share can be used as a noun or a verb:

Noun - part or portion of a larger amount which is divided among a number of people, or to which a number of people contribute.
Verb - have a portion of (something) with another or others.
Oxford state that balanced is an adjective: keeping or showing a balance; in good proportions.

So based on these concepts & definitions; the ideal of sharing our balanced park system has admirable. We have nearly 170 parks, 800+ Ha of land, 99 km of trails, 35 tennis court, 62 sports fields, 56 playgrounds plus lawn bowling, allotment gardens, a water Park, a skate park, 2 lacrosse boxes & more. Seems like we have more than enough parks & space to share right?

Seems we spend a lot of time talking to people about sharing. We seem to meet challenges when these concepts of sharing or balance come into play. I am reminded of what someone said recently that ‘depending on what seat you’re in’ can affect your perception about sharing or what the balance should be.

Finding that balance to share is a big part of what we do. How do we do it? We talk to people. We undertake planning exercises. We do surveys. We hold Open Houses. We post notices. We write blogs. We follow the OCP & Strategic Plans. We develop Parks, Recreation & Culture Master Plans.

This Master Plan goes to Council on July 9. It’s to give us direction on the next 5-7 years. We believe that it will help us continue our work to share a balanced park system.

Follow the link below for more information:
 http://www.saanich.ca/parkrec/masterplan.html

However, for the record, our actual revised draft Mission for the Department is

Working in partnership with the community, we create opportunities to enrich the community, sustain quality environments, and support healthy lifestyles.

I’m interested in your comments on this concept of SOS Parks – Share our Saanich Parks

Let me know what you think; please SHARE!

The Forest & the Trees

Saanich has a new Urban Forest Strategy. Last night Council unanimously approved it. “IT” was 18 months in the making. But some might say it’s been nearly 2 decades in the making. Saanich was one of the first municipalities to adopt a Tree Preservation Bylaw in 1993. Now we are taking the next step to create a sustainable, healthy community with the Urban Forest Strategy. We have engaged the community in a discussion to develop a long-term plan to achieve a sustainable urban forest.

We define the Urban Forest as:

“the sum total of all trees & their related ecosystems within the municipality.”

Urban forests play an important role in contributing to the environmental integrity and climate change resiliency of Saanich. They filter air and water, create shade, provide habitat and slow wind and storm water. Urban forests contribute to the social well-being of Saanich by providing healthy and enjoyable recreation opportunities, moderating local climate, shading homes and businesses to conserve energy, and are critical in mitigating the urban heat island effect. Urban forests contribute to the economic vibrancy of Saanich through increased property values and encouraging business on tree-lined streets.

The Saanich Urban Forest Vision is:

“The urban forest in Saanich is a highly valued asset comprised of a rich and diverse forest that is widely recognized and appreciated for the contribution that it makes to our community and to our sustainable environment.”

The goal is:

“Protect and Enhance the Urban Forest”

The seven Strategies are:

  • Grow the Urban Forest Canopy,
  • Amend the Tree Preservation Bylaw,
  • Develop Urban Forest Guidelines,
  • Integrate the Strategy with other Saanich & Regional Initiatives,
  • Inventory the Urban Forest,
  • Develop an Urban Forest Operations Program, and
  • Engage the Community.

There are several actions that support each of the strategy statements & over the next few years Parks staff along with other internal stakeholders will continue to work with the community to implant the vision of a rich, diverse & appreciated sustainable urban forest.

I want to thank the community, staff & our consultants for participating in the process. This process has reminded us all that Saanich is a community that cares & is passionate about their community & it’s Urban Forest.

Ch Ch Changes

David Bowie wrote about Ch Ch Change in 1971, Bob Dylan had already written about 'the times they are a- changin' back in 1964, Blind melon wrote Change in 1992. I am sure if I was to research artist, philosophers alike have written, talked or thought about CHANGE. In Saanich Parks, we are talking and living change.

Over the past few months Saanich has had 3 significant members of our management team retire. The first was our Planner extraordinaire, Russ Fuoco at the end of September. Saanich Parks will miss Russ as he was a tremendous support of parks and guided us through change with our role in the development process. However, we look forward to a continued great relationship with his successor, Sharon Hvodanski. Next in October, our own Ken Kreiger, Director of Parks & Recreation retired after 19 years with Saanich Parks & Recreation. Ken is tremendously well respected professional and a great believer in community development and partnerships.

Then before Ken's successor could even be named; Gerald Fleming, Parks Planning & Design Manager retired on Nov 25/09 after nearly 23 years with Saanich Parks. Gerald arrived in 1987 and at that time we had less than 90 parks. Over the next 23 years Gerald has had a hand in the development of nearly 80% of the current park system. We are now over 165 parks & 750 Ha of land. Under his guidance many new parks or existing parks were constructed or redeveloped. The list includes: McMinn, Beckwith, Brydon, Sierra, Rutledge and Gorge Waterway. He was also very involved in Natural Park/environmental projects such as Quick's Bottom, Glencoe Cove-Kwatsech, & Rithet's Bog. He also over saw Sport Park and facility improvements with soccer fields, the skate park & the redevelopment of the 2 lacrosse boxes. He was also instrumental in the development of the Galloping Goose, Lochside Trails, the Blenkinsop Trestle and Centennial Trails. His contribution to the positive change in our community is evident through the park system. The sharing of stories and the genuine appreciation from many of our park "Friends of" and Community Associations is a tribute to the respect he has from the community, staff and industry professionals.

So we are in the middle of change with staff as well as social, economic and environmental considerations we face as we approach 2010. Our park infrastructure will be changing thanks to Federal funding though the Recreation Infrastructure Canada program. The focus of the program will be on our Playgrounds, Sport Courts, Sports fields & Tennis Courts. Council will be soon considering a Bylaw to Regulate the Non-Essential Use of Pesticides along with a revision to the Council Policy on Integrated Pesticide Management. All the while we have continued to completed improvements along the Colquitz River, Mt. Douglas and Mt. Tolmie Parks as well as the Lake Hill Lawn Bowling Clubhouse project has begun.

So CHANGE is all around us, it's always easier when you initiate it but it's inevitable as the song says 'you got to change'. I am pleased for Russ, Ken and Gerald and miss them all greatly. However, I am excited about the future very much looking forward to working with our talented team of park staff to keep meeting the changing needs of our community.
